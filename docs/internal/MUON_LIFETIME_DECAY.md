# Internal Reference: Muon Lifetime and Decay Rates

This document provides precise information on muon lifetime and decay for use in the internal energetic/spiritual models (particularly the muon-catalyzed micro-reactions / "manna" framework).

## Rest Frame Lifetime

- **Mean lifetime (τ)** of a muon at rest: **2.1969811(22) × 10^{-6} seconds** ≈ **2.197 microseconds**.
- This is one of the most precisely measured particle lifetimes.
- The muon decays almost exclusively via the weak interaction:
  - μ⁻ → e⁻ + ν̄_e + ν_μ
  - μ⁺ → e⁺ + ν_e + ν̄_μ

The decay is a three-body process, so the electron/positron energy spectrum is continuous rather than monoenergetic.

## Relativistic Lifetime Extension (Time Dilation)

In the lab frame, moving muons live longer due to special relativity:

**Observed lifetime = γ × τ**

Where γ = 1 / √(1 − v²/c²)

### Examples

| Muon Energy | γ (Lorentz factor) | Effective Lifetime | Distance traveled (approx.) |
|-------------|--------------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| At rest     | 1                  | 2.197 µs           | ~660 meters (if moving at c) |
| 1 GeV       | ~9.5               | ~21 µs             | ~6.3 km                     |
| 10 GeV      | ~95                | ~209 µs            | ~62.6 km                    |
| 100 GeV     | ~950               | ~2.09 ms           | ~626 km                     |

**Note**: At ~660 meters (the distance mentioned in the beam model), even modestly relativistic muons (a few hundred MeV) can easily reach that distance before decaying.

## Implications for the Muon-Catalyzed Micro-Reactions Model

### 1. Need for Continuous or Repeated Production

Because even relativistic muons have limited lifetimes (microseconds to milliseconds depending on energy), sustaining a long-term "pops and clicks" process requires either:
- Continuous production of new muons along the path or volume, or
- A mechanism to regenerate or recycle muons (currently very difficult in practice due to the sticking problem in muon-catalyzed fusion).

This reinforces the model's emphasis on **distributed, ongoing processes** rather than a single injection of muons.

### 4. Decay Products

When a muon decays, it produces:
- An electron (or positron)
- Two neutrinos

In the internal model, the electron/positron from muon decay could be interpreted as:

## Practical Limitation: Muon Sticking Problem

In real muon-catalyzed fusion, a major obstacle is that the muon often becomes bound to the helium nucleus produced in fusion (sticking probability ~0.5–1% per fusion in deuterium-tritium mixtures). This removes the muon from further catalysis.

In the hypothetical model, this could be interpreted as:
- A natural "tax" or efficiency limit on the sustained process.
- Part of why the effect remains subtle and distributed rather than runaway or explosive.
- A requirement for continuous fresh muon input to maintain the "manna" flow.
