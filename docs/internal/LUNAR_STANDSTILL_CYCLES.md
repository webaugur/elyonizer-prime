## What Are Lunar Standstills?

The Moon's orbital plane is inclined by approximately **5.14°** to the ecliptic (the plane of Earth's orbit around the Sun). The points where the Moon's orbit crosses the ecliptic are called the **lunar nodes**.

The lunar nodes regress westward along the ecliptic with a period of approximately **18.6 years**. This regression causes the Moon's maximum and minimum declinations (angular distance from the celestial equator) to vary over an 18.6-year cycle.

### Major Lunar Standstill (~18.6 years)

- Occurs when the ascending node aligns such that the Moon's orbital inclination adds to Earth's axial tilt (23.44°).
- The Moon reaches its **maximum possible declination** of approximately **±28.5°** (23.44° + 5.14°).
- At this time, the Moon rises and sets at its most extreme azimuths (furthest north and south).
- There are "high Moons" (reaching higher altitudes) and "low Moons" (reaching lower altitudes) with greater variation than usual.
- This is the time of greatest monthly variation in the Moon's path across the sky.

### Minor Lunar Standstill (~9.3 years later)

- Occurs when the nodes are oriented such that the Moon's inclination subtracts from Earth's tilt.
- The Moon's maximum declination is reduced to approximately **±18.3°**.
- Rising and setting positions are less extreme; monthly variation in the Moon's path is minimized.

The full cycle from one major standstill to the next is ~18.6 years; minor standstills occur roughly halfway between them.

## Observational and Cultural Significance

### Extreme Rising and Setting Positions

During major standstills, the Moon can rise and set significantly further north or south than the Sun ever does. This creates striking visual alignments at certain latitudes and has been linked to megalithic monuments (e.g., possible alignments at Callanish Stones in Scotland, Chimney Rock in Colorado, and debates around Stonehenge).

### "High" and "Low" Moons

At major standstills, some full Moons ride very high in the sky while others stay unusually low. This affects moonlight duration, illumination patterns, and cultural interpretations.

### Relation to Eclipses

Standstills are related to but distinct from eclipse cycles:
- Eclipses occur when the Sun is near a lunar node (Saros cycle ~18.03 years).
- Standstills occur when the nodes are at specific orientations relative to the solstices.
- Major standstills often coincide with periods when eclipse seasons produce particularly extreme geometries.
