## Established Physics: Solar Gravitational Lensing

### The 1919 Eclipse Confirmation

One of the most famous tests of general relativity occurred during the total solar eclipse of May 29, 1919:

- Arthur Eddington and his team observed stars near the Sun's position in the sky.
- The Sun's gravity bent the starlight, causing apparent shifts in stellar positions (up to ~1.75 arcseconds at the Sun's limb, decreasing with angular distance).
- This confirmed Einstein's prediction that massive bodies curve spacetime and deflect light.

This was possible **only because of the total eclipse**: the Moon blocked the Sun's bright photosphere, allowing observation of stars that would normally be lost in solar glare.

### General Mechanism

According to general relativity, the deflection angle for light grazing a massive body is:

\[
\delta = \frac{4GM}{c^2 b}
\]

Where:
- \(G\): Gravitational constant
- \(M\): Mass of the lensing body (Sun in this case)
- \(c\): Speed of light
- \(b\): Impact parameter (closest approach distance)

For the Sun, this produces the well-measured deflection of ~1.75" at the limb.

## Lunar Gravitational Lensing

The Moon also lenses light due to its mass (~1/81 of Earth's). However:

- Its lensing effect is much weaker than the Sun's (roughly 1/27th the deflection for the same impact parameter, due to lower mass).
- During a total solar eclipse, both Sun and Moon are closely aligned from Earth's perspective.
- The combined Sun + Moon lensing is dominated by the Sun; the Moon's contribution is a small perturbation.

In practice, lunar lensing is negligible for most observations compared to solar lensing during eclipses.

## Observational Effects During Eclipses

### 1. Access to Stars Near the Sun

The primary practical effect is that total eclipses allow astronomers to observe stars at small angular separations from the Sun that are normally unobservable due to glare. This enabled the 1919 test and continues to be useful for certain astrophysical observations (e.g., studying the solar corona and nearby stellar fields).

### 2. Temporary Modification of Apparent Positions

During an eclipse, the positions of stars near the Sun appear slightly shifted due to gravitational deflection. This is a real, measurable change in the observational frame of reference for those distant objects.

From the perspective of an observer on Earth:
- The coordinate grid of the sky is subtly warped near the eclipsed Sun.
- This warping is strongest at the Sun's limb and falls off with angular distance.

### 3. Ionospheric and Atmospheric Effects

Eclipses also cause temporary changes in the ionosphere (reduced ionization) and local weather/temperature, which can indirectly affect radio propagation and some optical observations. These are secondary to the gravitational lensing but contribute to the overall change in the local "frame" during the event.
