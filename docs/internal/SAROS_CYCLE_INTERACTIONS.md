## What is the Saros Cycle?

The **Saros cycle** is an eclipse periodicity of approximately **18 years, 11 days, and 8 hours** (6,585.32 days or 223 synodic months). After one Saros period, the Sun, Earth, and Moon return to nearly the same relative geometry, and a similar eclipse occurs (though shifted in longitude by about 120°).

### Key Characteristics

- A single **Saros series** lasts 1,200–1,500 years and contains 69–87 eclipses (alternating between solar and lunar in some series, but solar and lunar have separate series).
- Each successive eclipse in a series is similar in type (total, annular, partial) and occurs at roughly the same time of year.
- Because of the extra 8 hours, each eclipse in a series is shifted westward by about 120° in longitude.
- At any given time, roughly 40–42 Saros series are active for solar eclipses (and a similar number for lunar eclipses).

The Saros arises from the near-commensurability of three periods:
- 223 synodic months (new moons)
- 239 anomalistic months (perigee returns)
- 242 draconic months (nodal returns)

This combination brings the Sun, Moon, and lunar nodes back into alignment.

## Interaction with the 18.6-Year Nodal Cycle (Lunar Standstills)

The Saros period (~18.03 years) is very close to, but slightly shorter than, the lunar nodal regression period (~18.6 years). This creates important long-term interactions:

- **Beat period**: The difference between 18.6 and 18.03 years produces a beat cycle of roughly **500–600 years** over which the relationship between eclipse seasons and standstill extrema slowly shifts.
- During periods when a Saros series coincides with major lunar standstills, eclipses can occur at more extreme declinations, producing visually dramatic or geometrically unusual events.
- The slow drift between Saros and nodal cycles means that the "character" of eclipses within a Saros series gradually changes over centuries (e.g., transitioning from high-latitude to equatorial eclipses).

In our model, this interaction between the Saros and nodal/standstill cycles generates complex, long-period modulation of the Earth-Moon-centered interference patterns.
