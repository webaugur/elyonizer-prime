## What is the Eclipse Year?

The **Eclipse Year** is the time it takes for the Sun to return to the same lunar node (the point where the Moon’s orbit crosses the ecliptic). Its length is approximately **346.62 days** (or more precisely 346.620075 days).

It is shorter than the tropical year (~365.24 days) because the lunar nodes regress westward along the ecliptic at about 19.35° per year. The Sun therefore meets the nodes again after traveling less than a full 360° relative to the fixed stars.

### Key Characteristics

- One Eclipse Year ≈ 346.62 days.
- In one Eclipse Year, there are usually two eclipse seasons (periods when eclipses can occur), separated by about half an Eclipse Year.
- Eclipse seasons last roughly 31–37 days each.
- The Saros and Inex cycles are built upon combinations of the Eclipse Year with other lunar periods (synodic and anomalistic months).

The Eclipse Year is one of the fundamental periods governing when eclipses can occur.
