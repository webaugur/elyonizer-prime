## Muon-Catalyzed Fusion Background (Real Physics)

Muon-catalyzed fusion is a real, experimentally observed process:

- A negative muon can replace an electron in a hydrogen molecule (e.g., deuterium or tritium).
- Because the muon is ~207 times more massive than an electron, the nuclei are brought much closer together (smaller Bohr radius).
- This dramatically increases the probability of quantum tunneling and fusion at relatively low temperatures.
- Each muon can catalyze a limited number of fusions before it either decays (~2.2 µs lifetime) or "sticks" to the reaction product (helium nucleus).

**Key Characteristics**:
- Reactions are individually very small (single fusion events).
- They can occur at or near room temperature/pressure under the right conditions.
- The process is inherently distributed and repetitive rather than explosive.
- Net energy gain has been difficult to achieve in practice due to the muon sticking problem and the energy cost of producing muons.

## Muon Sticking Probability (Key Limitation)

In real muon-catalyzed fusion, a major practical limitation is the **muon sticking probability** — the chance that the muon becomes bound to the helium nucleus produced in fusion and is removed from further catalysis.

### Key Data

- **Initial sticking probability** in d-t (deuterium-tritium) fusion: approximately **0.8–1.0% per fusion** (older measurements).
- **Effective sticking probability** (after accounting for muon reactivation/stripping from the alpha particle): typically reduced to around **0.5% or lower** under optimal conditions.
- As a result, a single muon can catalyze on average roughly **100–200 fusions** before being lost (due to sticking or natural decay), depending on temperature, density, and isotopic mixture.

This "sticking problem" is one of the primary reasons muon-catalyzed fusion has not yet achieved practical net energy gain, despite the reactions occurring at low temperatures.
