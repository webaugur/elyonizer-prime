## Overview of Milankovitch Cycles

Milankovitch cycles describe long-term variations in Earth’s orbit and axial orientation that affect the amount and distribution of solar radiation reaching Earth’s surface (insolation). They are primarily associated with glacial-interglacial climate cycles over tens to hundreds of thousands of years.

### The Three Main Cycles

1. **Axial Precession (Precession of the Equinoxes)**
   - Period: Approximately **25,772 years** (currently ~25,700–26,000 years).
   - Effect: The direction of Earth’s rotational axis slowly traces a circle, changing which hemisphere faces the Sun at perihelion. This shifts the timing of seasons relative to Earth’s closest approach to the Sun.
   - Impact: Alters the contrast between seasons, particularly affecting monsoon strength and ice melt timing.

2. **Obliquity (Axial Tilt)**
   - Period: Approximately **41,000 years**.
   - Effect: The tilt of Earth’s axis varies between about 22.1° and 24.5°.
   - Impact: Higher tilt increases seasonal contrast (hotter summers, colder winters); lower tilt reduces it. This strongly influences high-latitude summer insolation, critical for ice sheet growth or retreat.

3. **Orbital Eccentricity**
   - Period: Approximately **100,000 years** (with a secondary ~413,000-year cycle).
   - Effect: The shape of Earth’s orbit varies from nearly circular to more elliptical.
   - Impact: Higher eccentricity increases the difference in solar radiation between perihelion and aphelion, amplifying seasonal effects when combined with precession and obliquity.

These cycles do not act in isolation; their combined effects on insolation, especially at high northern latitudes during summer, are believed to be a primary driver of the glacial-interglacial cycles of the Pleistocene.
