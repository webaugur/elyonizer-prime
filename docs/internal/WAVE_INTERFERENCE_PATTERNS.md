## Basic Physics of Wave Interference

When two or more waves overlap in space and time, they interfere according to the principle of superposition. The resulting amplitude at any point is the vector sum of the individual wave amplitudes.

### Main Types

- **Constructive Interference**: Waves are in phase (peaks align with peaks, troughs with troughs). Amplitudes add, producing a larger resultant wave.
- **Destructive Interference**: Waves are out of phase (peaks align with troughs). Amplitudes subtract, potentially canceling completely.
- **Partial Interference / Beats**: When waves have slightly different frequencies, they produce periodic variations in amplitude (beats) as they move in and out of phase.
- **Standing Waves**: When waves of the same frequency traveling in opposite directions interfere, they create stationary patterns with fixed nodes (zero amplitude) and antinodes (maximum amplitude).
- **Diffraction and Pattern Formation**: Interference from multiple sources or through apertures creates characteristic patterns (e.g., double-slit experiment, diffraction gratings).

Interference is fundamental to quantum mechanics (wave functions), optics, acoustics, and electromagnetism. It underlies phenomena from noise-canceling headphones to the stability of atomic orbitals.
