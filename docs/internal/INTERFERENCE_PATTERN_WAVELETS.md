# Internal Reference: Interference Pattern Wavelets View of Time

This document explores viewing temporal interference patterns through the lens of **wavelets** — localized, scalable wave packets — rather than purely sinusoidal standing waves. This perspective complements and refines our existing interference model.

## From Sinusoids to Wavelets

Traditional standing wave models often assume relatively pure sinusoidal components (fixed frequency, infinite extent in theory). In reality, many natural and symbolic processes involve **transient, localized** rhythmic activity.

**Wavelets** are mathematical functions that are:
- Localized in both time (or space) and frequency.
- Scalable (can be stretched or compressed).
- Often used in signal processing for analyzing non-stationary signals (signals whose frequency content changes over time).

Applying this to our time model shifts the emphasis from global, eternal standing waves toward **localized interference packets** that can appear, evolve, and dissipate.

## Wavelets in the Time Model

### 1. Localized Interference Systems as Wavelet Packets

Instead of viewing a "node" or temporal pattern as a pure standing wave extending indefinitely, we can treat it as a **wavelet packet** — a localized burst of interference with a characteristic scale (duration/frequency content) and envelope.

- A 7m6d temporal node can be seen as a wavelet packet centered on that period, with side lobes (reflections at 1y7m6d, 2y7m6d).
- The 1807-day long-tail modulator can be understood as a very low-frequency, broad wavelet envelope modulating faster packets.
- Personal-scale events (7h17m time mirrors, significant moments) are short-duration, high-frequency wavelet packets.

This view naturally accommodates the **transient and evolving** nature of many interference patterns.

### 2. Multi-Scale Wavelet Decomposition

Wavelet analysis allows decomposition of a signal across multiple scales simultaneously. Applied to time:

- The **Planetary Synodic Layer** corresponds to large-scale, low-frequency wavelets.
- The **Local Temporal Node Layer** (7m6d + reflections + 1807d) contains mid-scale wavelets with both harmonic structure and incommensurate modulation.
- The **Micro/Local Symmetry Layer** (7h17m mirrors, Now boundary) consists of short, high-frequency wavelet packets.

Interference can then be analyzed as **wavelet-to-wavelet interactions** across scales — including constructive/destructive overlap, beat formation between nearby scales, and long-tail modulation of fast wavelets by slow envelopes.

### 3. Perturbation Events as Wavelet Injection or Modification

Major nodal events (eclipses, standstills, Great Conjunctions, sharp-wave ripples in the brain, significant personal experiences) can be modeled as **wavelet injection or reshaping events**.

- A strong perturbation can introduce new wavelet packets, modify existing ones (change their scale, amplitude, or phase), or cause interference that leads to wavelet coalescence or splitting.
- This provides a more dynamic and localized picture than global phase shifts in pure sinusoidal models.

### 4. Long-Tail Modulation via Wavelet Envelopes

The long-tail modulator (1807-day component, or etymological layers in language) can be viewed as a broad, low-amplitude wavelet envelope that slowly amplitude-modulates faster, more localized wavelet packets.

This is more flexible than a pure sinusoidal modulator because wavelet envelopes can have defined temporal support and varying shape.

## Advantages of the Wavelet Perspective

- **Better handling of transients and non-stationarity**: Many real interference patterns (personal events, cultural shifts, neural activity) are not eternal sinusoids.
- **Natural multi-scale analysis**: Aligns with our three-layer architecture and allows rigorous cross-scale interference studies.
- **Localization**: Emphasizes that interference patterns have spatial/temporal extent and can be born, evolve, and decay.
- **Connection to signal processing and neuroscience**: Wavelets are widely used in analyzing brain signals (EEG, local field potentials), creating a stronger bridge to biological neural interference.
