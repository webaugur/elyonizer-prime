## Historical and Scientific Background

### Rutherford Scattering (1911)

In 1911, Ernest Rutherford, Hans Geiger, and Ernest Marsden conducted the famous gold foil experiment:

- Alpha particles (helium nuclei, charge +2e, mass ~4 u) were fired at a thin gold foil.
- Most alpha particles passed straight through with little deflection.
- A small fraction were deflected at large angles, and some even bounced back toward the source.

**Key Conclusion**: Atoms consist of a tiny, dense, positively charged nucleus surrounded by mostly empty space. The positive charge and nearly all the mass are concentrated in the nucleus.

This experiment overturned the "plum pudding" model of the atom and established the nuclear model of the atom.

### Rutherford Scattering Formula

The differential cross-section for Coulomb scattering of alpha particles by a nucleus is given by the Rutherford formula:

\[
\frac{d\sigma}{d\Omega} = \left( \frac{Z_1 Z_2 e^2}{8\pi \epsilon_0 E} \right)^2 \frac{1}{\sin^4(\theta/2)}
\]

Where:
- \(Z_1, Z_2\): Atomic numbers of projectile and target
- \(E\): Kinetic energy of the alpha particle
- \(\theta\): Scattering angle

The strong angular dependence (\(1/\sin^4(\theta/2)\)) explains why most particles go straight through while a few are scattered at large angles.
